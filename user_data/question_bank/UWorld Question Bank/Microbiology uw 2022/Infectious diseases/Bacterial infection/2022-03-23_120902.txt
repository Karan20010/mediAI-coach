(Choice A) Lymphatic filariasis is caused by Wuchereria bancrofti, a roundworm transmitted
by mosquito bite. Although it can present acutely with adenolymphangitis (eg, tender
lymphadenopathy, fever), it typically affects the lower extremities and inguinal lymph nodes.
Diagnosis can be made by blood smear examination, which demonstrates microfilariae in the
circulation.

(Choice B) Toxoplasmosis is caused by Toxoplasma gondii, a protozoan transmitted by
ingestion of undercooked meat or contact with cat feces. Toxoplasmosis often presents with
lymphadenopathy and flu-like symptoms. However, gram-negative coccobacilli would not be
seen; instead, histopathology of affected lymph nodes may show rare parasites (eg,
bradyzoites).

(Choice C) Tuberculous lymphadenitis, a common extrapulmonary manifestation of
tuberculosis, is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which is transmitted via inhalation.
Although histopathology may show necrotizing granulomas, acid-fast bacilli would be
expected.

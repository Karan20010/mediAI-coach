(Choice A) Rheumatic fever is associated with the formation of cross-reacting antibodies to
renal, neuronal, and cardiac antigens following group A Streptococcus pharyngitis. Eggs
would not be seen in stool.

(Choice B) Hypersensitivity pneumonitis, an immune reaction to an inhaled agent, can occur
following exposure to mold dust or spores. Patients generally develop acute fever, nausea,
cough, and dyspnea. However, wheezing is uncommon, and eggs would not be shed in stool.

(Choice C) Chagas disease, a parasitic infection caused by Trypanosoma cruzi, can cause
myopericarditis during acute infection. However, cardiac and gastrointestinal manifestations
are much more likely to occur years or decades later. In addition, T cruzi is not shed in stool; it
can sometimes be visualized on blood smear.

(Choice E) Type IV hypersensitivity reactions are mediated by T cells and are often seen as
part of certain drug reactions (eg, Stevens-Johnson syndrome). In contrast, Ascaris infections
are associated with IgE-mediated (eosinophilic) hypersensitivity.
